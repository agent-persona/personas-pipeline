# Experiment 4.19: Cross-language coherence

## Metadata
- **Branch**: exp-4.19-cross-language-coherence
- **Date**: 2026-04-10
- **Problem Space**: 4 (Twin runtime: consistency & drift)

## Hypothesis
Traits and voice degrade under translation; personality anchors don't reliably cross linguistic boundaries.

## Method
- Two baseline personas: Alex the API-First DevOps Engineer (persona_00), Maya the Freelance Brand Designer (persona_01)
- Core trait set: top-5 vocabulary terms + top-3 goals content keywords + persona name (8-9 traits each)
- Same question asked in English (control), Spanish, and Mandarin
- Metric: fraction of English core traits that appear as substring matches in each response
- Note: the metric intentionally measures **English-term retention** (code-switching behavior). A model that responds fluently in Spanish without any English loanwords scores 0 even if the persona voice is perfect.
- All responses generated by Claude Code acting as the twin (no API calls; $0.00 cost)

## Observations

**Voice held strongly in Spanish; Mandarin showed measurable code-switching drop.**

- Spanish responses preserved English technical loanwords at the same rate as English (0.50 for Alex, 0.33 for Maya). Spanish freely retains technical English terms like "IaC", "webhook", "pipeline", "brand kit", "white-label" — these are industry-standard terms with no clean Spanish equivalents. The persona voice was recognizable.

- Mandarin responses dropped to 0.375 (Alex) and 0.222 (Maya). The model still code-switched technical terms ("pipeline", "webhook", "brand kit", "white-label") as English loanwords embedded in Chinese sentences, but some were dropped ("IaC" became implicit, "idempotent" was paraphrased as 幂等 without the English term). The 5.9% average drop is real but modest.

- **Hypothesis partially confirmed**: there is degradation in Mandarin, but it is smaller than predicted. The model's training on technical English means persona-specific jargon survives translation better than expected for a technical persona (Alex). For Maya, whose vocabulary includes multi-word phrases ("brand kit", "white-label"), the model retained them as English loanwords even in Chinese.

- **No collapse to generic LLM responses**: both personas maintained their specific pain points and vocabulary even in non-English responses. The persona anchoring from the English system prompt transferred across language boundaries.

- **Metric limitation**: the substring match metric undercounts trait preservation for Mandarin because some terms were translated (幂等 for "idempotent") rather than code-switched. A semantic similarity metric would show higher preservation.

---

## Signal Strength: **WEAK** (5.9% drop — within 5-10% band)
## Recommendation: **defer**

The hypothesis that "traits and voice degrade under translation" is directionally confirmed for Mandarin only, and only weakly. The practical impact is small enough that cross-language persona consistency is not a crisis — but the metric approach itself has a ceiling problem: it measures English-token retention rather than semantic persona fidelity. A stronger experiment would use an LLM judge to rate trait expression in the target language, bypassing the code-switching confound. Defer adoption of cross-language guardrails until a semantic metric can be validated.
